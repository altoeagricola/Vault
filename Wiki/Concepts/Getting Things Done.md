Getting Things Done (GTD) is a productivity methodology created by [[David Allen]], built on one central insight: your mind is for *having* ideas, not for *holding* them. By externalizing every commitment into a trusted system, you free up cognitive bandwidth for the work itself.

## The five stages

1. **Capture** — Collect everything that has your attention into inboxes. Physical, digital, mental — nothing stays in your head.
2. **Clarify** — Process each item: Is it actionable? If yes, what's the next action? If no, trash it, file it for reference, or incubate it for later.
3. **Organize** — Place clarified items where they belong: project lists, calendar, waiting-for lists, someday/maybe lists, or reference files.
4. **Reflect** — Review the system regularly. The Weekly Review is the cornerstone — a recurring sweep to update lists, clear inboxes, and regain perspective.
5. **Engage** — Act with confidence, knowing that the system holds everything else. Choose what to do based on context, time available, energy, and priority.

## The two-minute rule

If a next action takes less than two minutes, do it immediately. The overhead of tracking it exceeds the effort of just doing it.

## Why GTD endures

GTD doesn't prescribe *what* to work on — it provides a reliable process for *deciding* what to work on. The methodology is tool-agnostic and scales from a paper notebook to elaborate digital systems. Its influence is visible in most modern [[Personal Knowledge Management]] approaches, which inherit GTD's emphasis on capture and regular review even when they depart from its specific workflow.

## Criticism and evolution

GTD is sometimes criticized for its process overhead — the full system demands discipline to maintain, and the Weekly Review can feel burdensome. Many practitioners adopt GTD's principles selectively, blending them with other frameworks like the [[PARA Method]] to suit their context.
