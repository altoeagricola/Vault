Personal Knowledge Management (PKM) is the practice of systematically capturing, organizing, and retrieving information to extend individual cognitive capabilities. Rather than relying on memory alone, PKM treats knowledge as a living asset — something to be curated, connected, and compounded over time.

## Why it matters

The volume of information available today vastly exceeds what any person can hold in their head. PKM shifts the question from "how much can I remember?" to "how quickly can I find and use what I've already encountered?" A well-maintained system turns passive consumption into active knowledge — ideas that compound, resurface at the right moment, and connect across domains.

## Core principles

- **Capture** — Externalize thoughts and inputs before they fade. The system is only as good as what goes into it.
- **Organize** — Give captured material a home that reflects how you'll *use* it, not just where it *came from*. Methodologies like [[Getting Things Done]] and the [[PARA Method]] offer different lenses on this.
- **Connect** — Link ideas across topics. Isolated notes are trivia; connected notes are insight.
- **Retrieve** — Design for future access. If you can't find it when you need it, you don't have it.
- **Review** — Periodically revisit and prune. Knowledge goes stale; a system that isn't maintained decays.

## The LLM frontier

A more recent development is using language models as active collaborators in the PKM workflow — not just for search, but for ingestion, synthesis, and maintenance. [[Andrej Karpathy]]'s "LLM Wiki" concept exemplifies this: an AI agent that reads sources, extracts structured knowledge, and maintains an interlinked wiki on the owner's behalf. This shifts the human role from writer to curator and editor, dramatically increasing throughput without sacrificing coherence.
